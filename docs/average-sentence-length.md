# ASL

Average Sentence Length shows how much room I give an idea before letting the reader breathe.

## How is ASL calculated?

The formula is very simple:

```
ASL = (Total number of words) / (Total number of sentences)
```

Examples:

- A text with 120 words and 10 sentences: `ASL = 120 / 10 = 12`;
- A text with 250 words and 25 sentences: `ASL = 10`.

## Description

This score measures the average number of words per sentence. I use it as a quick signal for rhythm and clarity.

A low value usually means the text moves quickly. It can feel direct, sharp, and easy to follow. A high value usually means the text carries more thought per sentence. That can be useful for essays, analysis, and argumentation, but it can also become tiring if every sentence asks the reader to hold too much in memory.

This is not a score where “lower is always better”. Short sentences can be flat. Long sentences can be elegant. What matters is whether the sentence length matches the job of the text.

## How I use this to improve

When the score is high, I look for sentences that are doing two or three jobs at once. I can often improve the text by splitting the explanation, moving a side thought into a separate sentence, or turning a long chain of clauses into a cleaner sequence.

When the score is very low, I check whether the rhythm has become too choppy. Sometimes the cure is not simplification, but giving an important idea enough space to unfold.
