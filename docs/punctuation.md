# Punctuation

Punctuation shows the rhythm, fragmentation, and internal pressure of the text.

## How is it calculated?

Punctuation is expressed as a percentage of the total words in the text:

```
Punctuation Score = (Number of punctuation marks / Total number of words) × 100
```

This percentage shows how much punctuation appears, on average, per word, and is numerically identical to "per 100 words" normalization. Therefore, a value like "10" can be interpreted either as "10 punctuation marks per 100 words" or as "10% punctuation". 

## Description

This score measures how frequently punctuation marks appear relative to the number of words. I use it as a rhythm indicator.

A text with more punctuation may be more expressive, rhetorical, fragmented, conversational, or heavily structured. A text with less punctuation may feel calmer, flatter, cleaner, or more formal.

This metric is especially useful because punctuation often reveals habits I do not notice while writing: too many parentheses, too many dashes, too many rhetorical questions, too much ellipsis, or commas desperately holding together sentences that should have been sent to couples therapy.

## How I use this to improve

If punctuation is high, I look for places where the text is leaning too much on punctuation instead of structure. Parentheses may hide digressions. Dashes may hide sudden turns. Commas may hide sentences that need rebuilding.

If punctuation is low, I check whether the prose has become too monotonous. Sometimes a well-placed colon, dash, question, or parenthetical aside gives the text a more human pulse.
